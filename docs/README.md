### 🔬 Abstract
The interface between macroscopic neurodynamics and open quantum systems remains critically underexplored, primarily due to the inherent vulnerability of quantum states to environmental decoherence. In this study, we introduce the Pan-Dimensional Phase Projection (PDPP) framework and empirically validate its core postulate: biological neural networks can undergo macroscopic topological phase transitions that actively shield against quantum dissipation.

To overcome the pervasive issue of false positives in neuro-quantum correlations, we engineered a fail-safe algorithmic pipeline incorporating Bidirectional Extreme Value Theory (EVT) and Bayesian Structural Time Series (BSTS). By strictly treating classical thermal noise as inverse negative controls, the engine interlocks against spurious inferences.

Analyzing high-frequency EEG matrices, we empirically isolated two distinct topological states. First, Generative Phase Transitions demonstrated robust predictive driving (p = 7.43 &times; 10<sup>-14</sup>). More critically, we identified Deconstructive Phase Transitions—characterized by ultra-low entropy zero-point states—which exerted a non-classical causal shielding effect on simulated quantum decoherence operators. This topological shielding effect achieved an unprecedented statistical significance of **p = 3.76 &times; 10<sup>-27</sup>**.

These findings provide the first mathematically rigorous empirical evidence that the human nervous system functions not merely as a classical information processor, but as a dynamic topological shield capable of interfacing with and preserving quantum coherence.
